# Bibliography

## Textbooks

[1] Abbott, S. *Understanding Analysis*, 2nd ed. Springer, 2015. Chapters 2–4 cover limits of sequences and functions, continuity, and the topology of the real line. Exceptionally clear exposition with exercises that develop proof-writing skills. (Chapter 3)

[2] Abelson, H. and Sussman, G. J. *Structure and Interpretation of Computer Programs*, 2nd ed. MIT Press, 1996. Section 2.3.2 develops symbolic differentiation as a case study in recursive data structures; the direct ancestor of the approach used in Evenwicht. (Chapter 1)

[3] Abelson, H. and Sussman, G. J. *Structure and Interpretation of Computer Programs*, 2nd ed. MIT Press, 1996. Section 2.3.2 presents symbolic differentiation as a case study in recursive data structures. (Chapter 4)

[4] Abramowitz, M. and Stegun, I. A. *Handbook of Mathematical Functions*. National Bureau of Standards, Applied Mathematics Series 55, 1964. The classic reference for computational formulas, tables, and approximations. Chapter 6 (Gamma function), Chapter 7 (Error function), Chapter 26 (Combinatorics). (Chapter 2)

[5] Aho, A. V., Lam, M. S., Sethi, R., and Ullman, J. D. *Compilers: Principles, Techniques, and Tools*, 2nd ed. Pearson, 2006. Chapter 2 introduces syntax-directed translation and expression trees as abstract syntax trees. (Chapter 1)

[6] Alexander, C. K. and Sadiku, M. N. O. *Fundamentals of Electric Circuits*, 7th ed. McGraw-Hill, 2021. An accessible introductory text with extensive worked examples. Chapters 6–8 cover first-order and second-order circuits; Chapters 9–10 cover sinusoidal analysis and AC power. (Chapter 44)

[7] Anderson, R. M. and May, R. M. *Infectious Diseases of Humans: Dynamics and Control*. Oxford University Press, 1991. The foundational monograph linking mathematical epidemiology to empirical data, with detailed treatment of $R_0$, herd immunity, and vaccination. (Chapter 30)

[8] Andrews, G. E., Askey, R., and Roy, R. *Special Functions*. Cambridge University Press, 1999. Comprehensive treatment of Gamma, Beta, hypergeometric functions, and their applications. (Chapter 2)

[9] Angrist, J.D. and Pischke, J.-S. *Mostly Harmless Econometrics: An Empiricist's Companion*, 1st ed. Princeton University Press, 2009. The modern treatment of causal inference in regression; essential reading on omitted variable bias, instrumental variables, and the credibility revolution. (Chapter 17)

[10] Apostol, T. M. *Calculus, Volume 1*, 2nd ed. Wiley, 1967. Chapters 10–11 cover sequences, series, and power series with full proofs. A rigorous undergraduate reference. (Chapter 6)

[11] Apostol, T. M. *Calculus, Volume 1*, 2nd ed. Wiley, 1967. Chapters 1–10 develop the Riemann integral with full rigor, including the Darboux approach and the equivalence of upper/lower sums to Riemann sums. The standard reference for a rigorous yet accessible treatment. (Chapter 5)

[12] Archer, D. *Global Warming: Understanding the Forecast*, 2nd ed. Wiley, 2012. Clear exposition of the carbon cycle, greenhouse effect, and climate sensitivity aimed at a quantitative audience. (Chapter 38)

[13] Artin, E. *The Gamma Function*. Holt, Rinehart and Winston, 1964. An elegant 39-page monograph covering the Gamma function's essential properties with minimal prerequisites. (Chapter 2)

[14] Astrom, K. J. and Murray, R. M. *Feedback Systems: An Introduction for Scientists and Engineers*, 2nd ed. Princeton University Press, 2021. An accessible, modern treatment emphasizing the principles underlying feedback control. Available freely online. Particularly strong on interconnection of systems, frequency-domain methods, and fundamental limitations. (Chapter 29)

[15] Atkins, P. W. and de Paula, J. *Atkins' Physical Chemistry*, 11th ed. Oxford University Press, 2018. Chapters 20–22 cover chemical kinetics comprehensively: rate laws, temperature dependence, reaction mechanisms, and enzyme kinetics, with a clear exposition suitable for both chemists and applied mathematicians. (Chapter 34)

[16] Axler, S. *Linear Algebra Done Right*, 4th ed. Springer, 2024. A proof-oriented treatment that emphasizes linear maps over determinants. Particularly strong on inner product spaces and the spectral theorem. Available as open access. (Chapter 8)

[17] Axler, S. *Linear Algebra Done Right*, 4th ed. Springer, 2024. A rigorous treatment that emphasizes linear maps over matrices and develops the theory in a coordinate-free manner. Provides the theoretical depth complementing the computational focus of this chapter. (Chapter 9)

[18] Axler, S. *Linear Algebra Done Right*, 4th ed. Springer, 2024. An elegant treatment of linear algebra that emphasizes operators over determinants. Chapters 5 and 7 develop eigenvalue theory and the spectral theorem with particular clarity. (Chapter 10)

[19] Batchelor, G. K. *An Introduction to Fluid Dynamics*. Cambridge University Press, 1967 (reprinted 2000). The classic mathematical treatment of fluid mechanics. Rigorous derivations of Bernoulli's equation (Section 3.5), Poiseuille flow (Section 4.2), Stokes drag (Section 4.9), and the energy cascade (Section 6.8). (Chapter 43)

[20] Bate, R. R., Mueller, D. D., and White, J. E. *Fundamentals of Astrodynamics*. Dover, 1971. The classic introduction to orbital mechanics for engineers. Chapters 1–4 cover two-body motion, orbit determination, and orbital maneuvers. (Chapter 41)

[21] Battin, R. H. *An Introduction to the Mathematics and Methods of Astrodynamics*. AIAA, 1999. Rigorous mathematical treatment emphasizing Lambert's problem, regularization, and perturbation methods. (Chapter 41)

[22] Bertsekas, D. P. *Nonlinear Programming*, 3rd ed. Athena Scientific, 2016. A thorough treatment of both convex and nonconvex optimisation, with careful attention to optimality conditions, constraint qualifications and duality. Chapters 1–2 cover the unconstrained theory of this chapter. (Chapter 11)

[23] Bertsimas, D. and Tsitsiklis, J. N. *Introduction to Linear Optimization*. Athena Scientific, 1997. A rigorous and thorough introduction to LP, the simplex method, duality and sensitivity analysis. Chapters 1–5 cover the material of Part B of this chapter. (Chapter 12)

[24] Billingsley, P. *Probability and Measure*, 3rd ed. Wiley, 1995. A rigorous measure-theoretic treatment of probability, including careful proofs of the law of large numbers and Central Limit Theorem. (Chapter 13)

[25] Bishop, C. M. *Pattern Recognition and Machine Learning*. Springer, 2006. The standard graduate reference for probabilistic machine learning. Chapters 3–5 cover linear models, neural networks, and kernel methods with full derivations. The Bayesian perspective on regularization (ridge as Gaussian prior) is developed thoroughly. (Chapter 26)

[26] Boole, G. *A Treatise on the Calculus of Finite Differences*, 4th ed. Chelsea Publishing, 1958 (reprint of 1880 edition). The historical source for the operational treatment of difference operators. (Chapter 20)

[27] Box, G. E. P., Jenkins, G. M., Reinsel, G. C., and Ljung, G. M. *Time Series Analysis: Forecasting and Control*, 5th ed. Wiley, 2015. The foundational text for ARIMA modeling, applied here to demand forecasting. Chapter 9 treats seasonal models. (Chapter 32)

[28] Box, G.E.P., Jenkins, G.M., Reinsel, G.C., and Ljung, G.M. *Time Series Analysis: Forecasting and Control*, 5th ed. Wiley, 2015. The definitive treatment of the Box–Jenkins methodology; the foundational text for ARIMA modeling, model identification via ACF/PACF, and diagnostic checking. (Chapter 21)

[29] Boyce, W.E. and DiPrima, R.C. *Elementary Differential Equations and Boundary Value Problems*, 12th ed. Wiley, 2021. The standard undergraduate ODE text, covering first- and second-order equations, series solutions, Laplace transforms, and systems with extensive worked examples. (Chapter 19)

[30] Boyd, S. and Vandenberghe, L. *Convex Optimization*. Cambridge University Press, 2004. A modern treatment of optimization that makes extensive use of gradients and Hessians. Available freely at https://web.stanford.edu/~boyd/cvxbook/. (Chapter 7)

[31] Boyd, S. and Vandenberghe, L. *Convex Optimization*. Cambridge University Press, 2004. The definitive treatment of convex optimization theory and algorithms. Chapter 2 covers convex sets and functions; Chapter 3 covers convexity-preserving operations; Chapter 9 covers unconstrained minimization (gradient descent, Newton's method, self-concordance). Freely available at [https://web.stanford.edu/~boyd/cvxbook/](https://web.stanford.edu/~boyd/cvxbook/). (Chapter 11)

[32] Boyd, S. and Vandenberghe, L. *Convex Optimization*. Cambridge University Press, 2004. The modern reference for convex optimization, including LP as a special case. Chapters 4–5 develop duality theory in a unified convex framework. Available freely at the authors' website. (Chapter 12)

[33] Bracewell, R.N. *The Fourier Transform and Its Applications*, 3rd ed. McGraw-Hill, 2000. The standard reference for the continuous Fourier transform, its properties, and applications in physical science and engineering. Chapters on sampling theory and the DFT provide the bridge to discrete computation. (Chapter 22)

[34] Brealey, R. A., Myers, S. C., and Allen, F. *Principles of Corporate Finance*, 14th ed. McGraw-Hill, 2022. The standard MBA-level reference for capital budgeting, NPV, IRR, and corporate valuation. Chapters 2–6 cover the time value of money and investment rules. (Chapter 25)

[35] Brockwell, P.J. and Davis, R.A. *Introduction to Time Series and Forecasting*, 3rd ed. Springer, 2016. A mathematically rigorous introduction balancing theory and applications; especially clear on stationarity conditions and the Wold decomposition. (Chapter 21)

[36] Casella, G. and Berger, R.L. *Statistical Inference*, 2nd ed. Duxbury/Thomson Learning, 2002. Graduate-level treatment of distributional theory, sufficiency, and the relationships between distributions. (Chapter 14)

[37] Casella, G. and Berger, R.L. *Statistical Inference*, 2nd ed. Duxbury/Thomson Learning, 2002. The definitive graduate-level treatment of estimation and hypothesis testing. Chapters 7–9 cover point estimation, interval estimation, and hypothesis testing with full mathematical rigor. (Chapter 16)

[38] Chan, E. P. *Algorithmic Trading: Winning Strategies and Their Rationale*. Wiley, 2013. Practical guide to mean-reversion, momentum, and pairs trading with implementations. (Chapter 27)

[39] Chiang, A. C. and Wainwright, K. *Fundamental Methods of Mathematical Economics*, 4th ed. McGraw-Hill, 2005. A standard text for mathematical economics covering multivariate calculus with economic applications, including the Cobb–Douglas function and Lagrange multipliers. (Chapter 7)

[40] Chiang, A. C. and Wainwright, K. *Fundamental Methods of Mathematical Economics*, 4th ed. McGraw-Hill, 2005. An accessible introduction to optimization methods for economists. Chapters 12–13 cover Lagrange multipliers with extensive economic applications; Chapter 19 covers linear programming. (Chapter 12)

[41] Chung, F. R. K. *Spectral Graph Theory*. CBMS Regional Conference Series in Mathematics, No. 92. American Mathematical Society, 1997. The foundational monograph on spectral methods for graphs. Develops the theory of the normalized Laplacian, Cheeger inequalities, random walks, and graph isoperimetric inequalities. Mathematically rigorous; assumes graduate-level linear algebra. (Chapter 31)

[42] Corke, P. *Robotics, Vision and Control: Fundamental Algorithms in MATLAB*, 2nd ed. Springer, 2017. Provides algorithmic implementations closely aligned with the computational approach of this chapter. (Chapter 42)

[43] Cormen, T. H., Leiserson, C. E., Rivest, R. L., and Stein, C. *Introduction to Algorithms*, 4th ed. MIT Press, 2022. The standard reference for network flow algorithms (Ford-Fulkerson, Edmonds-Karp, push-relabel). Chapter 24 covers maximum flow and minimum cut. Chapter 22 covers graph representations and traversal algorithms. (Chapter 31)

[44] Cover, T. M. and Thomas, J. A. *Elements of Information Theory*, 2nd ed. Wiley-Interscience, 2006. The definitive graduate-level textbook on information theory. Chapters 2–4 cover entropy, relative entropy, mutual information, and the data processing inequality. Chapters 5 and 7 provide rigorous proofs of the source coding and channel coding theorems. Chapter 11 treats rate-distortion theory, and Chapter 12 covers maximum entropy. (Chapter 28)

[45] Craig, J. J. *Introduction to Robotics: Mechanics and Control*, 3rd ed. Pearson Prentice Hall, 2005. The standard undergraduate textbook. Chapters 2–4 cover kinematics and the DH convention; Chapter 6 covers dynamics; Chapters 8–9 cover trajectory planning and control. (Chapter 42)

[46] Csiszar, I. and Korner, J. *Information Theory: Coding Theorems for Discrete Memoryless Systems*, 2nd ed. Cambridge University Press, 2011. A mathematically rigorous treatment of multi-user information theory and coding theorems, suitable for researchers. (Chapter 28)

[47] Curtis, H. D. *Orbital Mechanics for Engineering Students*, 4th ed. Butterworth-Heinemann, 2020. Comprehensive undergraduate text covering two-body dynamics, orbital elements, Hohmann transfers, interplanetary trajectories, and the restricted three-body problem. (Chapter 41)

[48] De Prado, M. L. *Advances in Financial Machine Learning*. Wiley, 2018. Modern treatment of fractional differencing, backtesting pitfalls, and the deflated Sharpe ratio. (Chapter 27)

[49] DeGroot, M.H. and Schervish, M.J. *Probability and Statistics*, 4th ed. Pearson, 2012. A thorough treatment bridging probability and statistical inference, suitable for advanced undergraduates. (Chapter 13)

[50] DeGroot, M.H. and Schervish, M.J. *Probability and Statistics*, 4th ed. Pearson, 2012. Thorough undergraduate text covering all distributions in this chapter with applications. (Chapter 14)

[51] Diekmann, O. and Heesterbeek, J. A. P. *Mathematical Epidemiology of Infectious Diseases: Model Building, Analysis, and Interpretation*. Wiley, 2000. Rigorous treatment of the next-generation matrix method and the mathematical definition of $R_0$. (Chapter 30)

[52] Dirac, P.A.M. *The Principles of Quantum Mechanics*, 4th ed. Oxford University Press, 1958. The operator formalism for quantum mechanics, including bra–ket notation and commutation relations. (Chapter 23)

[53] Dorf, R. C. and Bishop, R. H. *Modern Control Systems*, 14th ed. Pearson, 2021. A comprehensive engineering text with extensive examples from mechanical, electrical, and aerospace systems. Excellent coverage of root locus, frequency response, and digital control. (Chapter 29)

[54] Durbin, R., Eddy, S. R., Krogh, A., and Mitchison, G. *Biological Sequence Analysis: Probabilistic Models of Proteins and Nucleic Acids*. Cambridge University Press, 1998. [55] Easley, D. and Kleinberg, J. *Networks, Crowds, and Markets: Reasoning about a Highly Connected World*. Cambridge University Press, 2010. An accessible interdisciplinary text suitable for undergraduates, combining graph theory, game theory, and market design. Available freely online. Particularly strong on the economic and strategic aspects of networks. (Chapter 31)

[56] Edelstein-Keshet, L. *Mathematical Models in Biology*. SIAM, 2005. Accessible introduction to mathematical biology including logistic growth, predator-prey models, and epidemic models. (Chapter 30)

[57] Elaydi, S.N. *An Introduction to Difference Equations*, 3rd ed. Springer, 2005. The standard graduate textbook covering linear and nonlinear theory, stability, bifurcation, and chaos in discrete dynamical systems. (Chapter 18)

[59] Ewens, W. J. *Mathematical Population Genetics I: Theoretical Introduction*, 2nd ed. Springer, 2004. [62] Feinberg, M. *Foundations of Chemical Reaction Network Theory*. Springer, 2019.
